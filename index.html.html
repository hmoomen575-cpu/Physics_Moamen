Meaning of Static Electricity

⚡ What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electrical charges or power. The charges could be in the form of electrons or ions.

Electricity is a secondary energy source — we get it from conversion of other sources like coal, natural gas, oil, or nuclear power.

🔋 Electric Charge

  • A physical quantity that can be transferred from one object to another
  • Charged objects exert forces on other charged and uncharged objects
  • Measured in Coulombs (C)
Two Types of Electric Charge:
➕ Positive Charge    ➖ Negative Charge
1
Conductors & Insulators

🔌 Conductors

Materials in which charges can move about freely

  • Metals (Copper, Silver, Gold)
  • Rich in free electrons
  • Allow electricity to flow easily

🚫 Insulators

Materials in which electric charge is not easily transported

  • Wood, paper, plastics, ceramics
  • Poor in free electrons
  • Block electricity flow
1
Static Electricity Definition
Definition: The electricity produced by friction (rubbing) is called frictional electricity or static electricity.

🔍 Key Points:

  • "Static" means stationary or not moving
  • Charges do NOT flow through the conductor
  • Charges only transfer from one substance to another
  • The material acquires positive or negative charge → called charged/electrified bodies
⚡ Electrostatics = Study of electrified bodies at rest
1
Charging by Rubbing
Material Rubbed With Charge Acquired Behavior
🔵 Polythene rod Woolen cloth ➖ Negative Attract each other
🟡 Perspex/Cellulose acetate Woolen cloth ➕ Positive Attract each other
⚫ Ebonite Fur ➖ Negative Attract each other
🟠 Glass Silk cloth ➕ Positive Attract each other
🟠 Glass Flannel cloth ➖ Negative —
💡 Important Note: The substance used for rubbing determines the charge, NOT the material type!
Example: Glass rubbed with flannel → Negative, but with silk → Positive

Electrostatic Induction

🔄 Process Steps:

  1. Start: Two neutral metal spheres touching each other
  2. Induce: Bring negatively charged rod close → electrons repelled to far side
  3. Ground: Connect wire to ground → free electrons flow out
  4. Remove: Remove wire first, then rod → sphere has uniform positive charge
🎯 Result: Attraction of uncharged object by charged object near it is due to electrostatic induction.

Dangers & Uses of Static Electricity

⚠️ Dangers

  • 🌩️ Lightning — damages buildings, harms people
  • 💻 Computer damage — sensitive components
  • 🔥 Fire ignition — flammable gases (petrol stations)
  • 🦠 Dust & germs — attracted to charged objects

✅ Uses

  • 🚗 Car painting — even coating with charged powder
  • 🖨️ Computer printers — toner distribution
  • 🏭 Smoke removal — electrostatic plates in chimneys
  • 🏥 Medical equipment — anti-static protection
2
Coulomb's Law

🔬 Coulomb's Experiment

Coulomb studied how the force between two charged spheres A and B depended on distance.

  • Measured force needed to twist suspending wire
  • Placed equal charges on spheres
  • Varied distance d between them
📊 Findings:
• Force varies inversely with square of distance: F ∝ 1/d²
• Force varies directly with product of charges: F ∝ q₁q₂
F = k × (q₁ × q₂) / d²
k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C² (proportionality constant)
q₁, q₂ = charges in Coulombs | d = distance in meters
2
Forces: Attraction & Repulsion

💥 Repulsive Force

When charges have same sign:

  • Both positive (++): REPEL
  • Both negative (−−): REPEL
  • Force points away from each other

🧲 Attractive Force

When charges have opposite signs:

  • Positive + Negative: ATTRACT
  • Force points toward each other
  • Newton's Third Law applies!
F₁ on₂ = −F₂ on₁
Equal in magnitude, opposite in direction
2
Solved Problem - Coulomb's Law

📝 Problem 1:

Two charges are separated by 3 cm. Object A has charge +6 μC, Object B has −3 μC. What is the force on Object A?

Solution:
F = k × (qₐ × qʙ) / d²
F = (9 × 10⁹) × (6 × 10⁻⁶ × 3 × 10⁻⁶) / (3 × 10⁻²)²
F = (9 × 10⁹) × (18 × 10⁻¹²) / (9 × 10⁻⁴)
F = 1.8 × 10² N (180 N)

📝 Problem 2:

Find distance between q₁ = 26 μC and q₂ = −47 μC for force magnitude of 5.7 N.

Solution:
d² = k × q₁ × q₂ / F
d² = (9 × 10⁹ × 26 × 10⁻⁶ × 47 × 10⁻⁶) / 5.7
d² = 1.93 m²
d = 1.39 m

The Electric Field

🌐 Definition

The electric field is the area around a charge in which the effect of that charge can be felt.

OR: A field defined by the magnitude of the electric force at any given point in space.

⚡ Properties:
  • Exerts force on other charges
  • Has magnitude and direction → Vector quantity
  • Measured by Electrometer
  • Field lines show direction a positive charge would move
4
Electric Field Intensity

📐 Formula

Electric field intensity (E) = Electric field lines per unit area

  • Varies with amount of charge
  • Decreases with square of distance from source
  • Depends on amount of charges and distance between them
E = F / q = k × q / d²
E = Electric field intensity (N/C)
F = Force (N) | q = Charge (C) | d = Distance (m)

📝 Example:

A force of 2 N acts on charge 4 μC. Calculate E.

E = F/q = 2 / (4 × 10⁻⁶) = 5 × 10⁵ N/C
5
The Electric Capacitor

🔋 Definition

Two parallel metal plates separated by an insulator (dielectric).

Used in: radio tuning, power filters, car ignition, camera flashes

When charged:
• One plate: +Q (Positive)
• Other plate: −Q (Negative)
• Creates potential difference (V) between them
Q = C × V
Q = Charge (Coulombs) | C = Capacitance (Farads) | V = Voltage (Volts)
5
Capacitor Connections

🔗 Series Connection

  • Charge: Q = Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃
  • Voltage splits: Vₜ = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
  • Equivalent: 1/Cₑq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃
If equal: Cₑq = C/n

🔗 Parallel Connection

  • Voltage: Vₜ = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
  • Charge splits: Qₜ = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃
  • Equivalent: Cₑq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃
If equal: Cₑq = n × C
5
Solved Problem - Capacitors

📝 Problem:

Find equivalent capacitance of network: 3μF, 11μF, (12μF series 6μF), all parallel, then series with 9μF.

Step 1: 12μF and 6μF in series
1/C' = 1/12 + 1/6 = 3/12 = 1/4 → C' = 4μF

Step 2: 3μF, 11μF, 4μF in parallel
C'' = 3 + 11 + 4 = 18μF

Step 3: 18μF series with 9μF
1/Cₑq = 1/18 + 1/9 = 3/18 = 1/6 → Cₑq = 6μF

🔢 Questions: Problems

📝 Problem 1:

Two charges q₁ = +4 μC and q₂ = −6 μC are separated by 2 cm. Calculate the electrostatic force between them.

Solution:
F = k × |q₁ × q₂| / d²
F = (9×10⁹) × (4×10⁻⁶ × 6×10⁻⁶) / (2×10⁻²)²
F = (9×10⁹) × (24×10⁻¹²) / (4×10⁻⁴)
F = 540 N (attractive)

📝 Problem 2:

A capacitor of 50 μF is connected to a 12V battery. Calculate the charge stored.

Solution:
Q = C × V
Q = 50×10⁻⁶ × 12
Q = 600 μC or 6×10⁻⁴ C

📝 Problem 3:

Two capacitors 6μF and 12μF are connected in series. Find the equivalent capacitance.

Solution:
1/Cₑq = 1/6 + 1/12 = 2/12 + 1/12 = 3/12 = 1/4
Cₑq = 4 μF

Unit 1 Summary

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Static Electricity: Charges at rest, produced by friction
  • Coulomb's Law: F = k·q₁·q₂/d² — force between point charges
  • Like charges repel, opposite charges attract
  • Electric Field: Area around charge where its effect is felt, E = F/q
